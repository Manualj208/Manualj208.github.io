Do You Need a Manual J to Pull HVAC Permits? Here's What to Know

March 26, 2026 12 min read

You're ready to replace your HVAC system. You contact a contractor, get a quote, and plan to start next week. Then someone mentions: "Did you pull a permit?" And if you did: "Did you submit Manual J?"

If you live in a place where permits are required, this becomes important. And the answer to "do I need Manual J?" is almost certainly yes.

Let's break down what's actually required, why, and how to make sure your permit gets approved the first time.

The Short Answer: Probably Yes

In most jurisdictions across the US, you need a Manual J to pull an HVAC permit. Some municipalities make it optional. Some make it mandatory. But almost everywhere, code officials want to see it.

The reason is simple: Building codes care about energy efficiency and safety. A Manual J proves that the system you're installing is:

  • The right size for your home (not oversized, wasting energy)
  • Properly calculated using industry standards (ACCA Manual J)
  • Safe and compliant with energy codes (IECC)
  • Designed by someone who knows what they're doing

Without Manual J, a code official has no proof you didn't just guess on the equipment size.

Which Jurisdictions Actually Require It?

This varies state to state and sometimes county by county. Here's the general landscape:

States that absolutely require Manual J in most places:

  • California (strict energy code requirements)
  • New York
  • Massachusetts
  • Illinois
  • Florida
  • Texas (larger cities)
  • Washington

States where it's mostly required:

  • Colorado, Minnesota, Ohio, Pennsylvania, Michigan, Georgia, North Carolina

States where it's sometimes required (depends on local jurisdiction):

  • Most others. It might be required in major cities but not rural areas.

The safest assumption: Your jurisdiction probably requires it. If you're pulling a permit, ask the permit office directly. They'll tell you if Manual J is required or recommended.

Most will say: "Wd want to see proof the system was properly sized." That proof is Manual J.

What Code Officials Are Actually Looking For

When a permit officer reviews your Manual J report, they're checking several things:

1. Methodology

Did the calculation use ACCA Manual J standards? Did someone use a proper tool (like Wrightsoft) or just guess?

A code officer can usually tell the difference between a legitimate report and something that was made up. Professional reports show their methodology. Fake ones don't.

2. Documentation

Room-by-room breakdown? Assumptions listed? Input values shown? A real Manual J report documents everything so anyone can verify it.

If the report is vague ("Home needs 50,000 BTU" with no explanation), a code officer will reject it. They need to see the work.

3. Compliance with Energy Codes

Does the report reference IECC (International Energy Conservation Code) standards? Does it show that the system was sized according to code?

Energy codes require equipment to be properly sized. Manual J proves you did this.

4. Professional Credentials

Who created this report? Is it signed by a qualified professional? Some jurisdictions want to see that an engineer or licensed HVAC contractor did the work.

That's why our reports are reviewed by actual HVAC expertsânot just spit out by software.

5. Ductwork Design (Manual D)

For the full package, code officials also want to see that ductwork was designed properly (Manual D) and equipment was selected correctly (Manual S).

A Manual J without Manual D is incomplete. If the ductwork isn't designed right, the system won't work even if it's the right size.

6. Relevance to Your Project

The calculation has to match your home. If you're replacing a furnace in a 2,000 sq ft house and the Manual J is for 5,000 sq ft, that's a problem.

Code officials check that the report is actually for your home, your address, your building.

What Happens If You Submit Without Manual J?

Depending on your jurisdiction:

Best case: The permit office asks for it. You have to get one before they'll move forward. This delays your project by days or weeks.

Worse case: They issue the permit based on what they know. Your contractor installs equipment. Then during final inspection, they ask for Manual J, see that the system is oversized or undersized, and fail the inspection. Now you have to redo the work.

Worst case: You never get a permit because you don't have Manual J. You install a system illegally. Later, if there's a problem with the system or if you sell your home, it comes back to haunt you. Insurance might not cover failures on unpermitted work.

Some contractors will tell you: "You don't need a permit, just trust me on the size." Don't trust that. In 2026, most jurisdictions require permits, and most of those require Manual J. It's not optional where it's required.

How to Make Sure Your Manual J Passes Inspection

If you're getting a Manual J, follow these steps to make sure it's inspection-ready:

Get It From a Qualified Source

Work with someone who knows HVAC and building codes. We use Wrightsoft (industry standard) and all work is reviewed by actual experts.

Don't use a $99 online calculator and call it done. It won't pass.

Make Sure It's Complete

The report should include:

  • Manual J (load calculation)
  • Manual D (ductwork design)
  • Manual S (equipment selection)
  • Room-by-room breakdown
  • Input assumptions documented
  • Professional review and signature

Verify It's for Your Home

Check that:

  • Your address is on it
  • Your square footage is correct
  • Your location/climate zone is right
  • It matches your actual home

A code official will notice if something's off.

Build It Early

Get your Manual J done before you pull permits. This gives you time to catch any issues and fix them before submission.

Don't scramble to get it done right before your contractor is supposed to start. You'll have problems.

Have Someone Explain It

If you have a question about the report, you should be able to call and ask. At Manual J USA, our experts can walk you through it.

If the service that created your report can't explain it, that's a red flag. Code officials might also ask for clarification.

Know Your Jurisdiction's Specifics

Some code offices have additional requirements. Some want to see specific forms. Some have preferred calculation methods.

Before you get a Manual J done, call your permit office and ask if they have any special requirements for HVAC load calculations.

Share those requirements with whoever's doing your Manual J. They can make sure the report meets them.

IECC Energy Code Requirements

The International Energy Conservation Code (IECC) is adopted by most states and many municipalities. It specifically requires:

Section 5-C101: Performance: Buildings must be designed to meet the energy code performance.

Section 5-C401.1: HVAC systems must be properly sized.

Section 5-C401.1.1: Load calculations must follow the ACCA Manual J methodology.

This is written into code. It's not optional. If your state or city has adopted IECC, you legally need a Manual J load calculation.

That's why code officials ask for it.

Common Reasons Permits Get Rejected (And How to Avoid Them)

1. System is Oversized

Manual J says 40,000 BTU but contractor proposed 60,000 BTU. Code office rejects it as oversized. Equipment wastes energy.

How to avoid: Make sure equipment selection (Manual S) actually matches the load. Don't let contractors upsell you on bigger equipment.

2. No Manual D (Ductwork Design)

You have a load calculation but no ductwork design. Code office wants to see how you're distributing that heating and cooling.

How to avoid: Get all three manualsâJ, D, and Sâdone together.

3. Input Data Wrong

The calculation assumed R-30 insulation in the attic, but you actually have R-1u/ System was oversized based on wrong assumptions.

How to avoid: Verify all data with your contractor before submitting. Double-check square footage, window details, insulation levels.

4. Report Isn't for Your Home

The Manual J is for 123 Main Street. Your home is 456 Oak Drive. Code officer notices the mismatch.

How to avoid: Confirm the report has your correct address and matches your actual home.

5. Report Lacks Documentation

It's just a number with no explanation. Code officer can't verify the methodology.

How to avoid: Use a professional service that provides complete documentation and input assumptions.

6. Professional Credentials Missing

The report isn't signed by anyone qualified. No PE (Professional Engineer),no HVAC contractor license, no credentials.

How to avoid: Work with someone who has industry credentials and can sign the report professionally.

7. Doesn't Match Current Code

An old Manual J from 2010 might not meet 2026 energy standards. Code office wants current work.

How to avoid: Get a fresh Manual J done for your current project.

Who Needs to Pull the Permit?

In most cases, your contractor pulls the permit. Some jurisdictions allow homeowners to pull their own. Ask your permit office.

Either way, they'll need the Manual J report. Make sure your contractor has it (or plan to provide it yourself).

If you're hiring a contractor, ask:

  • Do they typically get Manual J calculations?
  • Do they pull permits?
  • What documents do they need from you?

A good contractor knows they need Manual J for permits. If they say they don't, find a different contractor.
